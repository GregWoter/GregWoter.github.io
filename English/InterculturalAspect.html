    Personal Experiences

    Here I give two examples of cultural differences that I have experienced. An explanation is given why this would cause conflict in professional interactions.

    Greeting

    The first cultural difference I experienced in Belgium is the way people greet each other. For example, the first time I was working a vacation job, my coworkers came up to me really closely and tried to go in for cheek kisses while I stood there and held out my hand. This created some tension with some coworkers because they took offense. In Belgium, cheek kissing is a common greeting to relatives or friends. Here, in the Flemish part of Belgium, usually three kisses are exchanged. Usually women will kiss both women and men, while men will kiss women but withstand from kissing other men. Coming from a mixed household(a father from Nigeria(Africa) and a mom from Belgium(Europe)), I was not used to cheek kissing as a greeting. I have only ever known to just make verbal greetings or to shakehands with relatives and friends.

    If this form of greeting is used in other countries in professional interactions where it’s custom to give each other space while greeting – for example, in Japan, greeting people is expressed with bowing – it could cause conflicts in professional interactions, because the gesture of cheek kissing is reserved for family or couples. A way to cope with such differences in greetings is to do your research on the culture of the country you will be staying in and will have professional interactions in.

    Facial expressions

    Prior research revealed that facial expressions are a hard-wired behavior with evolutionary origins. But there are a studies that challenge this idea. These studies say that facial expressions are affected by culture and have a different meaning in these certain cultures. This may create a certain social barrier between people with cultural differences and will bring certain problems to the table.

    For example Europeans rely on the eyebrows and mouth to express emotions while asians will rely on the eyes. These cultural differences can lead to misinterpreted signals duringprofessional interactions.
